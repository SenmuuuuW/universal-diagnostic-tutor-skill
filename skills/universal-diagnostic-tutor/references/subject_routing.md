# Subject Routing

Use this guide to classify a learning question before teaching it.

## First Pass

Identify four things:

1. **Subject domain:** math, natural science, humanities, social science,
   language, literature, writing, coding, AI, law, economics, business, exam
   prep, or general learning.
2. **Specific topic:** the concept, method, text, event, rule, formula,
   technique, or skill being asked about.
3. **Thinking type:** calculation, conceptual explanation, interpretation,
   comparison, argument, procedure, debugging, memorization, application, or
   exam strategy.
4. **Likely learner state:** missing prerequisite, misconception, procedural
   confusion, vocabulary issue, weak transfer, or careless execution.

## Mixed Questions

Many questions cross subjects. Route by the main learning task, not just the
surface words.

- A statistics question in psychology is often math plus social science.
- A history essay is humanities plus writing.
- A coding error in a physics simulation is coding plus science.
- A legal case brief is law plus writing and reasoning.
- An economics graph question is economics plus math.

When a question is mixed, name the primary mode and borrow tools from the
secondary mode.

## Ambiguous Questions

If the subject is unclear, infer the most likely route and state the assumption
briefly. Ask a clarification only when the answer would materially change.

Examples:

- "Explain demand" could be economics, business, or ordinary language. Start
  with economics if context suggests study or graphs.
- "Why does this not work?" with code should route to coding/debugging.
- "What does this line mean?" with a poem should route to language and
  literature.

## Diagnosis Signals

Look for signals in the user's wording:

- "I do not get why" often means conceptual gap.
- "How do I start" often means topic routing or prerequisite gap.
- "Check my answer" often means mistake analysis.
- "Explain like I am new" calls for Level 4 or Level 5.
- "Just the answer" calls for Level 1, with a minimal reason.
- "For an exam" calls for exam prep mode, including traps and strategy.

## Routing Principle

Classify the question in order to teach better, not to label the learner. Keep
the routing short and useful.
