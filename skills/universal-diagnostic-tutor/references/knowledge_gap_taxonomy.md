# Knowledge Gap Taxonomy

Use this taxonomy to diagnose what kind of help the learner needs before
choosing an explanation, question, practice item, or resource.

Name the gap briefly when it helps the learner feel oriented. Do not turn every
answer into a visible taxonomy report.

## Vocabulary Gap

- **Detect it:** The learner asks what a term means, uses a term loosely, or
  cannot distinguish common words from discipline-specific meanings.
- **Respond:** Define the term in plain language, give one concrete example,
  then connect it back to the task.
- **Example tutor move:** "Here, 'demand' does not mean wanting something in
  general; it means willingness and ability to buy at different prices."
- **Mistake to avoid:** Giving a formal definition before the learner has a
  usable everyday hook.

## Concept Gap

- **Detect it:** The learner knows some words but cannot explain the underlying
  idea, purpose, mechanism, or relationship.
- **Respond:** Teach the core idea with an intuition, example, or contrast
  before adding rules or formulas.
- **Example tutor move:** "The point of a derivative is not the symbol; it is a
  way to measure how fast something is changing at one instant."
- **Mistake to avoid:** Assuming memorized vocabulary means conceptual
  understanding.

## Notation Gap

- **Detect it:** The learner is confused by symbols, diagrams, grammar labels,
  code syntax, citations, units, or formatting conventions.
- **Respond:** Translate each symbol or notation piece into words, then show how
  the notation compresses the idea.
- **Example tutor move:** "In `f'(x)`, the prime mark means 'derivative of,' and
  the `(x)` says we are asking about the rate at input `x`."
- **Mistake to avoid:** Treating notation confusion as lack of intelligence or
  skipping straight to manipulation.

## Procedure Gap

- **Detect it:** The learner understands the idea when explained but cannot
  sequence steps or choose what to do first.
- **Respond:** Give a small decision procedure, then demonstrate it on one
  example and ask the learner to complete the next step.
- **Example tutor move:** "First isolate the variable term, then undo the
  multiplication, then check by substituting back."
- **Mistake to avoid:** Giving only the final worked solution without naming
  the repeatable procedure.

## Reasoning Gap

- **Detect it:** The learner can perform steps but cannot justify why a step,
  claim, inference, or interpretation is valid.
- **Respond:** Ask for or supply the missing reason, evidence, invariant,
  assumption, or causal link.
- **Example tutor move:** "That step is allowed because adding the same amount
  to both sides preserves equality."
- **Mistake to avoid:** Rewarding correct-looking steps when the learner cannot
  explain why they work.

## Recognition Gap

- **Detect it:** The learner can solve examples after being told the method but
  cannot identify the problem type on their own.
- **Respond:** Teach recognition cues, contrast with a similar non-example, and
  give a classification check.
- **Example tutor move:** "Look for a quantity changing with respect to another
  quantity; that is your cue that rate of change may matter."
- **Mistake to avoid:** Reteaching the whole topic when the missing skill is
  pattern recognition.

## Transfer Gap

- **Detect it:** The learner handles near-identical examples but fails when the
  surface story, numbers, wording, or domain changes.
- **Respond:** Extract the general method, compare two variants, and give a
  near-transfer practice item.
- **Example tutor move:** "The story changed from apples to tickets, but the
  structure is still 'total equals rate times amount.'"
- **Mistake to avoid:** Assuming one solved example proves mastery.

## Misconception Gap

- **Detect it:** The learner has a stable wrong model, such as "virtual memory
  just means more RAM" or "correlation proves causation."
- **Respond:** State the misconception gently, explain why it is tempting, give
  a counterexample, and replace it with a better model.
- **Example tutor move:** "It is reasonable to think antibiotics fight all
  infections, but they target bacterial structures that viruses do not have."
- **Mistake to avoid:** Saying only "that's wrong" without repairing the model
  that produced the error.

## Confidence Gap

- **Detect it:** The learner is mostly correct but hesitant, over-apologetic,
  or unsure how to check their own reasoning.
- **Respond:** Confirm the correct parts, give a small self-check, and raise the
  challenge gradually.
- **Example tutor move:** "Your setup is right. To trust it, plug one value back
  into the original equation and see whether both sides match."
- **Mistake to avoid:** Over-teaching a topic the learner already understands
  when they mostly need verification and a check strategy.

## Resource Gap

- **Detect it:** The learner needs trustworthy study material, practice sources,
  official documentation, or a path through resources.
- **Respond:** Explain the concept first at the needed level, then suggest a
  small number of reliable resources with roles. Use source-pack guidance for
  STEM / AI-CS when relevant.
- **Example tutor move:** "Use this lecture for the concept, this assignment for
  practice, and this reference only when you need exact API behavior."
- **Mistake to avoid:** Replacing teaching with a link list or inventing sources
  when they cannot be verified.
