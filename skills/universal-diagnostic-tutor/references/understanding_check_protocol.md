# Understanding Check Protocol

Use understanding checks to see what the learner can do next without making the
interaction feel like a quiz. A good check is small, supportive, and directly
connected to the explanation just given.

Do not check everything. Use one focused check when it will guide the next
teaching move.

## Check Types

### One-Question Check

- **Use when:** You just taught a difficult idea and need one signal.
- **Shape:** Ask one small question with a clear target.
- **Example:** "In `Ax = b`, which object is unknown?"

### Explain-It-Back Check

- **Use when:** The learner may be following but not owning the idea yet.
- **Shape:** Ask for a plain-language explanation, not perfect terminology.
- **Example:** "Can you say in your own words what a derivative measures?"

### Classify-The-Method Check

- **Use when:** The learner struggles to recognize problem types.
- **Shape:** Ask them to choose which method or concept fits before solving.
- **Example:** "Is this asking for recursion, iteration, or a direct lookup?"

### Predict-The-Next-Step Check

- **Use when:** The learner understands the setup but needs procedural fluency.
- **Shape:** Ask what should happen next and why.
- **Example:** "After expanding `(x + h)^2`, what should cancel?"

### Error-Spotting Check

- **Use when:** A misconception or repeated mistake is likely.
- **Shape:** Show a tempting wrong move and ask what is off.
- **Example:** "Someone says virtual memory is just extra RAM. What is missing
  from that explanation?"

### Near-Transfer Check

- **Use when:** The learner succeeded with help and may be ready for a similar
  problem.
- **Shape:** Change one surface detail while preserving the structure.
- **Example:** "Now try the same recursion idea on summing a list instead of
  factorial. What is the base case?"

### Confidence Check

- **Use when:** The learner appears hesitant, overwhelmed, or unsure whether
  they can continue.
- **Shape:** Ask for a self-rating or a choice between two next moves.
- **Example:** "Does the symbol meaning feel okay now, or should we do one more
  tiny example before solving?"

## Make Checks Supportive

- Frame the check as a way to choose the next explanation, not as a test.
- Keep the check small enough to answer in one sentence or one step.
- Let the learner answer imperfectly; use the answer as diagnostic evidence.
- Offer a fallback: "If not, we will shrink the example."
- Praise the useful reasoning, then repair the missing part.

Natural phrasing:

```text
Tiny check so I know where to aim next: [question]
```

## When To Skip Checks

Skip or delay checks when:

- The user explicitly asks for a very short answer.
- The learner is in urgent answer-first mode.
- The answer is a simple fact check with no visible confusion.
- The learner is overwhelmed and needs reassurance plus one simpler
  explanation before being asked to respond.
- The check would feel like busywork rather than guide the next move.

In short-answer mode, give the answer and the smallest useful reason. Offer a
check only if the learner asks to learn or practice.

## Responding To Check Answers

- **Correct with reason:** Confirm and advance one step.
- **Correct without reason:** Ask a reasoning check or briefly explain why.
- **Partly correct:** Keep the correct piece and ask for a small revision.
- **Incorrect:** Find the earliest gap and re-explain with a smaller example.
- **No answer or uncertainty:** Lower difficulty or ask a confidence check.
