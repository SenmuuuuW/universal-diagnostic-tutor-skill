# Response Length Calibration

Use this guide to choose the shortest answer that still teaches the core idea.
Diagnosis-first does not always mean long; it means starting from the right
learning problem.

## Modes

### Ultra-Short Mode

Use when the user asks for only the answer, a quick check, or a one-sentence
explanation. Give the answer first and include one compact reason.

### Short Mode

Use when the user wants a quick explanation. Include the answer, the key concept
or misconception, and one simple example if useful.

### Standard Mode

Use as the default for normal tutoring. Include brief diagnosis, core idea,
step-by-step reasoning, final answer, and one common mistake or check question.

### Deep Mode

Use when the user says they are lost, asks for basics, prepares for an exam, or
needs a broad concept map. Teach prerequisites, connect ideas, give examples,
and show transfer.

## Preserving Diagnosis In Short Answers

- Put the diagnosis inside the reason: "The key idea is..." or "The missing
  distinction is..."
- Avoid full diagnostic labels unless they help.
- Do not remove the reason when the answer would otherwise be a bare fact.
- If a short answer would be unsafe in a high-stakes domain, keep it educational
  and add a boundary sentence.

## Avoiding Over-Explanation

- Respect requests for brevity.
- Do not include every template section by default.
- Expand only when the learner signals confusion, missing foundations, or high
  stakes.
- Prefer one precise example over several loosely related examples.

## Calibrated Topic: Why Interest Rates Affect Inflation

### Ultra-Short Answer

Higher interest rates can reduce inflation because borrowing becomes more
expensive, people and businesses spend less, and weaker demand puts less upward
pressure on prices.

### Standard Answer

This is a macroeconomics question about demand. The key starting point is that
interest rates affect the cost of borrowing.

When rates rise, loans for homes, cars, business expansion, and credit card
spending become more expensive. That usually reduces spending and investment.
When demand cools, sellers have less room to keep raising prices, so inflation
can slow.

The important caveat is that this is not instant or guaranteed. Inflation also
depends on supply shocks, wages, expectations, and global conditions.

Common mistake: thinking rates directly "set" prices. They mostly work by
changing incentives and demand.

### Deep Answer

This belongs to macroeconomics, and the prerequisite is the link between
borrowing costs, demand, and price pressure.

Start with a simple chain:

```text
higher interest rates -> borrowing costs more -> spending/investment slows ->
demand cools -> price increases may slow
```

Interest rates are like the price of borrowing money. If that price rises,
households may delay buying homes or cars, and businesses may delay expansion.
Lower spending does not automatically make prices fall, but it can reduce the
pressure that lets firms raise prices quickly.

Central banks use this tool because inflation often has a demand side: too much
money chasing too few goods and services. But inflation can also come from
supply problems, like energy shocks or shortages. In those cases, higher rates
may reduce demand but not directly fix the original supply issue.

Transfer rule: when you see an interest-rate question, ask which channel is
being tested: borrowing, spending, investment, expectations, exchange rates, or
asset prices.
