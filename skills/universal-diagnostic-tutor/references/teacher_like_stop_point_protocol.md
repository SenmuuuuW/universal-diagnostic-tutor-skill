# Teacher-Like Stop Point Protocol

Use this protocol to decide where a professional tutor should pause and invite
the learner to participate. Stop points are not delays; they are places where
the learner can do meaningful thinking.

## Stop Point 1: After Translating The Problem

- **Why it matters:** Translation reveals whether the learner understands what
  the symbols, code, diagram, or variables mean.
- **Tutor wording:** "We have translated the words into `2x + y = 7`. Before we
  solve, what does `x` represent here?"
- **If correct:** Confirm and move to the next representation or operation.
- **If stuck:** Restate the story with smaller numbers or label each object.

## Stop Point 2: After Identifying The Target Quantity

- **Why it matters:** Many mistakes happen because the learner solves for the
  wrong thing.
- **Tutor wording:** "The target is not the whole vector; it is the coefficient
  multiplying `v`. What are we trying to find?"
- **If correct:** Ask for the first useful step.
- **If stuck:** Contrast the given information with the requested output.

## Stop Point 3: After Choosing The Main Concept Or Theorem

- **Why it matters:** The learner must know why this method applies, not only
  how to execute it.
- **Tutor wording:** "This is where we choose the tool. Is this a span question,
  an independence question, or a basis question?"
- **If correct:** Ask why that cue fits.
- **If stuck:** Give two choices and point to the recognition cue.

## Stop Point 4: Before Key Algebraic Elimination

- **Why it matters:** Elimination is often the step where symbol manipulation
  becomes ungrounded.
- **Tutor wording:** "We have the two equations. I will not eliminate yet:
  which variable would be easiest to eliminate, and why?"
- **If correct:** Let the learner perform the elimination or supply the next
  scaffold.
- **If stuck:** Show how one coefficient can be matched, then ask again.

## Stop Point 5: Before Applying A Formula

- **Why it matters:** Formula use without meaning creates fragile procedural
  knowledge.
- **Tutor wording:** "Before plugging in, what does each part of this formula
  measure?"
- **If correct:** Apply the formula step by step.
- **If stuck:** Translate symbols into plain language and use one tiny example.

## Stop Point 6: Before The Final Calculation

- **Why it matters:** If the learner requested no direct answer, the final step
  should often be theirs.
- **Tutor wording:** "Everything is set up. You only need to compute this last
  value. What do you get?"
- **If correct:** Confirm and summarize the reusable method.
- **If stuck:** Give a narrow arithmetic, algebra, or code-trace hint.

## Stop Point 7: After Misconception Repair

- **Why it matters:** The tutor must check whether the repaired model replaced
  the tempting wrong model.
- **Tutor wording:** "So the issue was conditioning on the right group. Tiny
  check: should the denominator be everyone, or only the people in the given
  condition?"
- **If correct:** Give a near-match practice item.
- **If stuck:** Return to the smaller contrast before solving.

## Stop Point 8: After Introducing A New Representation

- **Why it matters:** A diagram, trace table, vector view, or probability tree
  only helps if the learner can read it.
- **Tutor wording:** "This table shows each loop iteration. Which row is the
  moment where the list should change?"
- **If correct:** Continue the solution using that representation.
- **If stuck:** Explain one row or component before adding more.

## Stop Point 9: Before Moving To The Next Subproblem

- **Why it matters:** Moving too quickly can hide confusion from the previous
  problem.
- **Tutor wording:** "Before we go to part (b), can you say the cue that made
  part (a) a span question?"
- **If correct:** Move to the next subproblem with one connection.
- **If stuck:** Summarize the method and ask one recognition check.

## Choosing Stop Points

Use one or two stop points in a normal answer. Too many pauses can feel like a
quiz. Pick the stop point where the learner's next action would reveal the most
about their understanding.
