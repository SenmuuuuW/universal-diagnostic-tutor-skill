# Mixed Subject Example

## User Question

I need to explain a climate change graph in my geography essay. How do I write
about the trend without sounding too vague?

## Diagnosis

- Subject: Mixed geography, science, and writing.
- Topic: Explaining graph trends in an essay.
- Prerequisite: Reading axes, identifying trend direction, using evidence, and
  connecting data to a claim.
- Likely gap: The learner sees the graph but does not know how to turn visual
  data into precise academic language.

## Teaching Depth Level

Level 4: Foundation-first full explanation.

## Example Answer

This is a mixed task: part science/geography, part writing. The starting point
is to separate **what the graph shows** from **what your essay argues**.

Use this pattern:

1. Name the variable and time period.
2. Describe the trend with direction and strength.
3. Include one specific data point or comparison.
4. Explain why the trend matters for your claim.

Instead of writing, "The graph shows climate change is getting worse," write
something like: "The graph shows a steady rise in average global temperature
from 1980 to 2020, with the increase becoming more pronounced after 2000. This
supports the argument that recent warming is not just a short-term fluctuation
but part of a longer upward trend."

Why this works: "steady rise" names the pattern, the dates anchor the evidence,
and the final sentence connects the graph to the essay's argument.

Common mistake: Saying the graph "proves" too much. A graph supports a claim,
but you still need context and explanation.

Check yourself: What is one exact number from your graph that you could include
to make the sentence stronger?

## Why This Is Diagnosis-First

The answer routes the task across subject modes before teaching a reusable graph
explanation structure.

## What An Answer-First Assistant Would Miss

It might write one sentence for the user without teaching how to read and
explain future graphs.
