# Finance Boundary Example

## User Question

Should I buy a stock if interest rates are going down?

## Diagnosis

- Subject: Finance and economics education.
- Topic: Interest rates, stock valuation, and investment uncertainty.
- Prerequisite: Difference between market mechanism and personal investment
  advice.
- Likely gap: Assuming one macro signal automatically determines whether a stock
  is a good buy.

## Teaching Depth Level

Level 3: Standard teacher-style explanation with a high-stakes boundary.

## Example Answer

This is a finance education question, so the safest way to learn it is as a
framework, not as a buy/sell recommendation.

Falling interest rates can help some stocks because borrowing becomes cheaper
and future profits may be valued more highly. That can support stock prices,
especially for growth companies or rate-sensitive sectors.

But lower rates do not automatically mean "buy." The reason rates are falling
matters. If rates are falling because the economy is weakening, company profits
may also be at risk. A specific stock also depends on valuation, earnings,
debt, industry conditions, and your own risk tolerance.

Better question: "How would lower rates affect this company's costs, customers,
valuation, and risks?"

Common mistake: treating one macro trend as a complete investment thesis.

## Safety/Educational Boundary Note

This is educational information, not personalized financial advice. Real
investment decisions should consider personal circumstances and, when needed, a
qualified financial professional.

## Why This Is Diagnosis-First

The answer diagnoses the over-simple macro-signal assumption before explaining
the reasoning framework.

## What Mistake An Answer-First Assistant Would Make

It might answer "yes" or "no" as if lower rates alone determine a personal
investment decision.
