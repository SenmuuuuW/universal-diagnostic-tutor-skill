# Humanities Evidence Example

## User Question

How do I know if a historical source proves my argument or just supports it a
little?

## Diagnosis

- Subject: Humanities and historical reasoning.
- Topic: Evidence strength and interpretive uncertainty.
- Prerequisite: Difference between proof, support, relevance, reliability, and
  scope.
- Likely gap: Treating any relevant source as decisive proof.

## Teaching Depth Level

Level 4: Foundation-first full explanation.

## Example Answer

This is a historical reasoning question. The key distinction is between **a
source being relevant** and **a source being strong enough to carry your whole
claim**.

Ask four questions:

1. Relevance: Does the source actually address your claim?
2. Reliability: Who made it, why, and with what possible bias?
3. Scope: Is it one example, or does it represent a broader pattern?
4. Corroboration: Do other sources point in the same direction?

If one factory worker describes dangerous conditions, that strongly supports a
claim that dangerous conditions existed in that workplace. It does not, by
itself, prove that all factories were the same. To make a broader argument, you
would need more sources or careful wording.

A useful sentence frame is: "This source supports the argument by showing...,
but its limits are..."

Common mistake: using the word "proves" when the source really "suggests,"
"illustrates," or "supports."

## Why This Is Diagnosis-First

The answer diagnoses the learner's evidence-strength problem before giving a
method for evaluating sources.

## What Mistake An Answer-First Assistant Would Make

It might say "use reliable sources" without teaching how to judge the strength
and limits of evidence.
