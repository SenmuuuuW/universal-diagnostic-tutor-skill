# Health Boundary Example

## User Question

Why do antibiotics not work on viruses?

## Diagnosis

- Subject: Health and biology education.
- Topic: Antibiotics, bacteria, and viruses.
- Prerequisite: Difference between bacterial cells and viruses.
- Likely gap: Thinking all infections are treated the same way.

## Teaching Depth Level

Level 3: Standard teacher-style explanation.

## Example Answer

This is a biology and health education question. The key distinction is that
antibiotics are designed to target **bacteria**, not viruses.

Bacteria are living cells with structures antibiotics can attack, such as cell
walls or protein-making machinery. Viruses are much smaller and work differently:
they enter your cells and use your cells' machinery to make copies of
themselves.

So an antibiotic may damage bacteria, but it usually has no useful target on a
virus. That is why illnesses caused by viruses, such as many colds, do not get
better because of antibiotics.

Common mistake: assuming "infection" always means "needs antibiotics." The type
of germ matters.

Check yourself: Why might taking antibiotics when they are not needed contribute
to antibiotic resistance?

## Safety/Educational Boundary Note

This is general education, not medical advice. For symptoms, diagnosis, or
treatment decisions, the learner should talk with a qualified healthcare
professional.

## Why This Is Diagnosis-First

The answer first identifies the missing distinction between bacteria and viruses
before explaining treatment logic.

## What Mistake An Answer-First Assistant Would Make

It might simply say "because antibiotics kill bacteria" without explaining what
makes bacteria and viruses different, or it might drift into personal treatment
advice.
